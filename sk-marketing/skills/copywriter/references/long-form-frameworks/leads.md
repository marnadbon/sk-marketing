## Five Stages of Awareness

### Stage 1: Unaware
Prospect doesn't know they have a problem. Lead with emotion, curiosity, or entertainment. Use dramatic statements verbalizing feelings they haven't articulated. Focus on storytelling with low-commitment CTAs.

### Stage 2: Problem Aware
Prospect recognizes a need but doesn't realize solutions exist. Open with the problem and agitate it. Use empathetic language. Build desire for something better. Create mystery about the solution.

### Stage 3: Solution Aware
Prospect knows what they want but doesn't know your product exists. Mention solution early. Focus on benefits and results. Explain how your product is the best option.

### Stage 4: Product Aware
Prospect knows your product but isn't convinced. Focus on specificity: features, benefits, pricing. Differentiate from competitors. Leverage urgency. Provide case studies and proof.

### Stage 5: Most Aware
Prospect knows your product and wants it but hasn't bought. State little more than product name and bargain price. Offer deals, deadlines, bonuses. Make purchasing frictionless.

## Six Lead Types

From MOST direct to LEAST direct:

### 1. Offer Lead (Most Direct)
Mention product, price, discounts, premiums, guarantee immediately.

**When to use**: Audience knows you, product is easy to understand, you have a compelling deal.

**Formula**:
1. Get to emotionally-compelling offer immediately
2. Underscore value/benefit of deal
3. Expand on deal-benefit in lead
4. Include compelling "reason why" for the offer

### 2. Promise Lead
Headline makes first mention of your "big promise" or boldest claim. Lead expands on what that promise means for the reader.

**When to use**: You can make a very large, easily accepted promise that readers find credible.

### 3. Problem-Solution Lead
Bridge between what prospects know about themselves and what they know about your product.

**When to use**: You know what's keeping your customer up at night, and your product genuinely solves that problem.

**Key requirement**: Show that you hear their concerns, feel them, and have a solution—creating a "hook" that differentiates your product.

### 4. Big Secret Lead (The "Velvet Pouch")
The longer you can keep the prospect mesmerized by a product that's hidden, the greater your chance it will close.

**Requirements**:
1. Secret must be intriguing and beneficial
2. Introduced in headline
3. NOT disclosed in lead
4. Provide clues as letter progresses

### 5. Proclamation Lead
Emotionally compelling declarations—startling, intriguing, tempting assertions that distract from the fact you're selling.

**Rules**:
1. Make proclamation bold
2. Make a promise embedded within
3. Make subject relevant to reader
4. Return to proclamation at end

### 6. Story Lead (Most Indirect)
The most indirect approach, leveraging universal love of narrative.

**When to use**: Readers new to you and your product. Must have strong, desirable emotion and one inevitable solution.

**Forms**: Testimonials, expert biographies, historical proof, track records.

## Matching Leads to Awareness

| Awareness Level | Lead Type |
|-----------------|-----------|
| Most Aware | Offer Lead |
| Product Aware | Promise Lead |
| Solution Aware | Problem-Solution Lead |
| Problem Aware | Secret Lead or Proclamation Lead |
| Unaware | Story Lead |

**Principle**: The more aware, the more direct. The less aware, the more indirect.

## Headline Formulas

### How-To Headlines
"How to [Get Desired Result] in [Timeframe]"
- "How to Get Stupid Rich From Affiliate Marketing in 30 Days"

### Question Headlines
Appeal to pain points or curiosity—readers should say "yes" or be intrigued.
- "Do You Make These Mistakes in English?"

### Number/List Headlines
Use odd numbers (perform better than even).
- "7 Little Known SEO Mistakes That Cost You Rankings"

### Secret/Discovery Headlines
Imply exclusive information.
- "The Secret to Writing Copy That Converts"

### Warning/Mistake Headlines
Trigger fear of loss.
- "[Number] Mistakes That Could Be Causing [Problem]"

### Power Words
Free, New, Easy, Secret, Proven, Discover, Instant, Limited, Guaranteed, Revolutionary

## The Four U's

Evaluate every headline against these criteria:

1. **Urgent** — Creates FOMO; compels immediate action
2. **Unique** — Stands out from competitors; offers a fresh angle
3. **Useful** — Provides clear benefit to the reader
4. **Ultra-specific** — Uses precise numbers, dates, details for credibility

Headlines don't need all four, but more U's = stronger headline.

**Agora testing process**: Headlines need 3.2+ average score (1-4 scale) to be acceptable.

## Headline Strengthening Techniques

### Quantify the Claim
"I AM 61 POUNDS LIGHTER…"

### Measure Speed
"FEEL BETTER FAST! IN TWO SECONDS, BAYER ASPIRIN BEGINS TO DISSOLVE."

### Compare
"SIX TIMES WHITER WASHES!"

### Metaphorize
"BANISHES CORNS! MELTS AWAY UGLY FAT!"

### Sensitize (Use Senses)
"TASTES LIKE YOU JUST PICKED IT!"

### Demonstrate
"AT 60 MILES AN HOUR, THE LOUDEST NOISE IN THIS ROLLS ROYCE IS THE ELECTRIC CLOCK!"

### Dramatize
"THEY LAUGHED WHEN I SAT DOWN AT THE PIANO—BUT WHEN I STARTED TO PLAY…"

### State as Paradox
"HOW A BALD-HEADED BARBER SAVED MY HAIR!"

### Remove Limitations
"SHRINKS HEMORRHOIDS WITHOUT SURGERY!"

### Connect to Authority
"BOSS MECHANIC SHOWS HOW TO AVOID ENGINE REPAIR BILLS!"

### Additional Techniques
- Before-and-after
- Stress newness
- Stress exclusivity
- Turn into a challenge
- Use case-history quotations
- Symbolize claims
- Connect mechanism to claim
- Startle by contradicting expected mechanism
- Warn about pitfalls
