## Three Types of Proof

Strong promotions use ALL three types:

### Factual Proof
- Research-based evidence
- Statistics and data
- Expert studies
- Medical or scientific journals
- Appeals to logic, establishes objective credibility

### Anecdotal Proof
- Stories (factual and illustrative)
- Shows rather than tells
- Appeals immediately to emotions
- Very powerful for engagement—humans process narrative more readily than data

### Social Proof
- Testimonials from customers
- Expert endorsements
- Celebrity associations
- Customer numbers ("Join 10,000+ satisfied customers")
- Case studies of real results

## Bencivenga's Eleven Proof Elements

Every claim must pass the **"Yeah, Sure" test**—the skeptical internal filter readers apply.

1. **Demonstration** — Show the product working
2. **Creative Guarantee** — Reverse risk completely
3. **Reason Why** — Explain logic behind claims
4. **Specifics** — Concrete numbers, dates, details
5. **The Exposé** — Reveal hidden information
6. **Explain the Mechanism** — Show how it works
7. **Sell Against Type** — Unexpected positioning
8. **Acknowledge Disbelief** — Address skepticism directly
9. **Highly Believable Source** — Expert credentials
10. **Testimonials** — Customer proof
11. **Candor** — Honest admission of limitations

**Bencivenga's warning**: "Never make your claim bigger than your proof. Always join your claim and your proof at the hip in your headlines."

## Fascinations and Bullets

Bullet points designed to conjure curiosity by teasing value, embellishing benefits, using extreme verbiage, and withholding the full answer.

### Classic Examples (Schwartz/Bencivenga)

> "On page 33, you discover a six-second motion with your mouth that instantly relaxes the over tense muscles of your chest, heart, and solar plexus—and drains hypertension out of your body like water running from a sieve"

> "How to drink with a client (or friend) and never have him notice you're dieting. See page 164."

> "When you have a painful callus or corn, peel it right off using nothing more than lemon juice, tea and garlic. Just follow the directions on page 230."

> "Press here to relieve dizziness any time. See page 280."

> "The simple massage technique that breaks the 'knots' of tissue in painful joints and increases your range of pain-free motion. See page 101."

### Blind vs. Revealed Bullets
- **Blind bullets**: Tease benefit without giving mechanism (majority of bullets)
- **Revealed bullets**: Occasionally give away the secret for credibility balance
- **Best practice**: Vary between blind and revealed

### 11 Tips for Effective Bullets

1. Keep them short (3-4 lines maximum)
2. Vary bullet length throughout the section
3. Make them benefit-oriented: "Because it has [FEATURE], you will be able to [ADVANTAGE]. What that means to you is [BENEFIT]"
4. Add facts, figures, and cited studies
5. Include testimonials when relevant
6. Avoid rounded numbers (use "$11,750" not "$10,000")
7. Research similar product reviews for bullet ideas
8. Group bullets by categories with subheads
9. Include question bullets to engage readers
10. Tease to something only answered by buying
11. Write fascination bullets that pique curiosity

## Social Proof and Testimonials

Basecamp saw 102% increase in conversions with homepage testimonials.

### Six Types of Social Proof

1. **Customer testimonials** (most versatile—include full name, photo, company, role)
2. **Expert endorsements** (authority figures with credentials)
3. **Celebrity endorsements** (leveraging existing fan trust)
4. **User reviews/ratings** (star ratings and written reviews)
5. **Case studies** (detailed success stories: problem, solution, results)
6. **Social metrics** (number of customers, media mentions)

### Testimonial Best Practices
- Match testimonials to objections being addressed
- Include specific results and numbers
- Organize by benefit type
- Place strategically throughout copy
- Use full name, photo, company, role when possible

## Guarantee Structures

Risk reversal works because:
- People hate losing more than they like gaining
- Fear of bad decisions paralyzes action
- Guarantees communicate product confidence

### Types of Guarantees

**Money-back guarantee** (most common)
- 30-day, 60-day, 90-day, 1-year, lifetime
- Counterintuitively, longer guarantees = fewer refunds (people forget)

**Performance guarantee**
- Promises specific measurable results
- "Double your sales in 60 days or work for free"
- Higher risk but higher conversion

**Conditional guarantee**
- Requires customer action to qualify
- Puts implementation risk on customer

**"Keep the bonuses" guarantee**
- Allow product return while keeping bonuses
- Adds extra value

**Free trial/try before buy**
- Virtually eliminates risk
- The "take the puppy home" strategy

### Guarantee Wording
- Word in "positive terms" that assume satisfaction
- Make risk-free and unconditional
- Examples: "10x Rate Guarantee," "90-day full refund, no questions asked"

## Objection Handling

### Five Universal Objections

**No Need**
- Solution: Clear USP and demonstrating problem severity through pain points

**No Trust**
- Solution: Credentials, testimonials, case studies, guarantees, transparent communication

**No Money**
- Solution: Value stacking, payment plans, ROI demonstration, comparing to cost of inaction

**No Hurry**
- Solution: Urgency, scarcity, deadlines, fear of missing out

**No Desire**
- Solution: Better research, emotional connection, deeper motivations

### Preemptive Objection Handling
Raise objections before readers do: "You might be thinking..." then address directly with honesty and solutions.

## Offer Construction

Write out the offer BEFORE writing the sales letter.

### Price Positioning Strategies

**"Mundane product" comparison**
- Compare investment newsletter to cost of morning coffee

**Value-first presentation**
- Present value before price

**Alternative comparison**
- Compare to college courses, consulting fees

### Value Stacking
1. List all components with individual values
2. Show total value versus actual price
3. Stack bonuses and resources
4. Create perceived value far exceeding price point

### Urgency and Scarcity

**Types of scarcity**:
- **Limited quantity**: "Only 5 items left" or "Limited to first 100 customers"
- **Limited time**: "Sale ends in 24 hours" with countdown timers
- **Exclusive access**: "Members only," "Early access for subscribers"

**Urgency language**: Now, Today, Immediately, Limited, Last Chance, Before, Deadline, Fast, Hurry, "Don't miss out," "While supplies last," "Almost gone"

**Ethical guideline**: Use only real scarcity—false claims damage trust.
